Frequently Asked Questions

This website story's twist involves Helen of Sparta, the most beautiful woman in the world. She had kids with the prince of Troy (Paris), and once King Menelaus finds Helen, he offers to spare her life at the cost of killing her two perfect children with Paris. To which she agrees.

A memorable character in the story is Paris, because had he been a regular person with no power, there would have been no Trojan war.

An alarming gift is the apple of discord because while the apple was a “gift” to the fairest, it was a trick as well to start an argument among the three goddesses.

In this case, Eris’s actions of spite or revenge are ultimately what causes distress upon the mortals and the start of the trojan war. Fate plays a role in Eris’s actions because she is the reason for all the conflict that will later impact the future of many others.

Eris, embodiment of chaos and represents competition. Fuels competition Aphrodite, goddess of love, lust and beauty. Can make people fall in love Athena, Goddess of wisdom and warfare. Stands by men and gives strength/wisdom Hera, goddess of marriage and childbirth, queen of the Olympians. Paris, a Trojan prince. Mortals have no divine power.

Menelaus is our story's original hero for saving his wife (Helen of Sparta), but Menelaus could be considered a villain for how he acted once finding Helen in Troy. He risks the lives of many men in the name of retrieving his wife, whom he originally intended to kill. This implies that King Menelaus would sacrifice innocent lives to fulfill an act of murder. It is not justifiable.
